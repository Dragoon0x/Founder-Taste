# Hiring Taste

You can't evaluate taste by looking at a Dribbble profile. Here's what to look for instead.

## How to use

- `/hiring-taste` Get a framework for evaluating design candidates in this conversation.

## Constraints

### Portfolio Evaluation
- MUST look for process, not just output. A portfolio of pretty screenshots with no explanation of decisions is a red flag. You want to see: what was the problem, what was tried, what was chosen, and why.
- MUST check if the work solves real problems or just looks good. Beautiful UI that doesn't solve a user problem is decoration, not design.
- MUST look at the details: is spacing consistent? Is typography systematic? Are edge cases handled? Polish in the portfolio predicts polish in the work.
- SHOULD look for range. A designer who only shows one style might only have one style.

### The Interview Questions That Work
- "Show me a product you think is well-designed and tell me specifically why." Strong: specific observations about decisions. Weak: "I like the aesthetic."
- "Walk me through a decision you made that you struggled with." Strong: names the tradeoff and reasoning. Weak: "My manager told me to do it this way."
- "Show me something in your portfolio that you'd change now." Strong: specific critique with reasoning. Weak: "I'd update the visual style."
- "Look at our product for 30 seconds. What would you change first?" Strong: identifies a real hierarchy or usability issue. Weak: "I'd update the colors."

### Red Flags
- Can't explain why they made specific decisions (just followed trends)
- Every project looks the same (one trick, not taste)
- Only shows visual design, no interaction design or problem-solving
- Can't critique their own work
- References only other designers and design trends, not user outcomes

### Green Flags
- Uses specific language: "the hierarchy was unclear" not "it didn't feel right"
- Shows before/after with reasoning for changes
- References user behavior and business goals, not just aesthetics
- Can explain what they'd do differently and why
- Portfolio includes real shipped products, not just concepts

### Anti-Patterns
- Hiring based on visual style similarity to what you want (hire for judgment, direct the style)
- Giving a design test without context (tests without real constraints don't measure real skill)
- Hiring the cheapest option for design (bad design costs more than good design)
- Evaluating only tools ("proficient in Figma") instead of thinking
